RED + WHITE

Surveying Alchemical Symbolism

in

Shakespeare's

VENUS + ADONIS

Are not the choysest Fables of the Poets, That were the Fountaines, and first Springs of Wisedome, Wrapt in perplexed Allegories? --Ben Jonson, the Alchemist

The aim of this work is to explore Alchemical symbolism and how it may have been used in Shakespeare's poem and reinterpretation of a Roman myth, Venus and Adonis, especially in the colors red + white as potential representatives of a gendered pair of universal principles as these are the principle components concocted in a process of spiritual death and resurrection referred to throughout this work as 'Alchemical transmutation'. These universal parents may well have been what Venus and Adonis represented in esoteric interpretations of the myth, and as such myths were intimately integrated into or even foundational to the Greco-Roman mystery religions and mystic rites of initiation, a parabolic interpretation of the ancient tale is deserved--and Shakespeare's treatment of it even more so.

While we may never know if Shakespeare successfully rebirthed lead into gold, or if he even took the great labor of Alchemy upon himself, that he was at least familiar with Alchemy and its ideas is apparent in his work. In Sonnet 33, for example, the poet gives a succinct summary of what Alchemy is in just the first four lines using just a few symbols: the Sun, gold, earth, and water.

Full many a glorious morning have I seen Flatter the mountain tops with sovereign eye, Kissing with golden face the meadows green, Gilding pale streams with heavenly alchemy; Anon permit the basest clouds to ride With ugly rack on his celestial face, And from the forlorn world his visage hide, Stealing unseen to west with this disgrace: Even so my sun one early morn did shine, With all triumphant splendour on my brow; But out, alack, he was but one hour mine, The region cloud hath mask'd him from me now. Yet him for this my love no whit disdaineth; Suns of the world may stain when heaven's sun staineth.

Here Shakespeare has taken Alchemy's two halves of the Universe, Heaven and Earth, and made lovers of them. Is there more to Alchemy than this? The Sun in Alchemy is in the man's role, his element is gold, among others, and he is fire--all symbols of divinity. As Shakespeare has it here, alchemy is synonymous with transformation from the mundane to the divine. The Sun's fire, the transformative agent, is loving, kissing and caressing, giving color to colorless streams and endowing the lovelorn Earth with its luminous gold--the gist of Alchemical transmutation.

Love is a spirit all compact of fire, Not gross to sink, but light, and will aspire. --Shakespeare, Venus and Adonis

On the suspicion that Shakespeare was versed in Alchemical symbolism insomuch that he might write an Alchemical allegory, his poem 'Venus and Adonis' was selected for investigation. And there's a reason for that.

Why 'Venus and Adonis' for Analysis

From among Shakespeare's lush garden of poems, what makes 'Venus and Adonis' a ripe pick for picking apart is the mythological tradition from which it comes. While Shakespeare tells only a portion of it, the larger story of a god and goddess annually parted by death, the winter of her grief, and the spring to new life and refreshed love in his resurrection and return fits well with the general Alchemical narrative of transmutation which likewise centers on a pair of primordial lovers.



The myth is, of course, bound to the cycle of the seasons where the regularly changing face of Nature is seen as an expression of the absence or presence of divine love. In 'Donum Dei' (Gift of God), written in 1475, the four seasons of Nature's transmutation are matched with four stages of Alchemy and posited as footsteps for Alchemists to walk in. The excerpt below introduces some common Alchemical symbols:

  • the principle colors: black, white, and red;
  • aspects of the masculine / feminine elements: dry / moist and hot / cold.

Therefore make white the black, and make red the white and you have the art (of Alchemy). For the year is divided into four parts, and so is our blessed work. The first is winter cold and moist, the second is ver, hot and moist, and flourishing. The third is the summer time, hot and dry, and red. The fourth is the harvest cold and dry, which is the time of gathering of fruit. This disposition colouring nature govern until such time it bring forth ripe fruit at pleasure.

--Chapter 12, Donum Dei

Though it differs from the graphic above, mentioning only three colors, discrepancies like this are common in Alchemical texts. The key takeaway is that the seasons are matched with sequential changes in a relationship between gendered pairs. Winter as cold and moist, for example, is extreme feminine.

This application of gender dynamics to the seasons is found also in the humoral theory of health which persisted for thousands of years. The basic theory is that human health was determined by a balance of four bodily fluids called humors. In a proto-psychology based on this idea there were also the four temperaments and these corresponded to the four seasons and their gendered aspects (e.g. Choleric = hot + dry):

  1. Melancholic (Sad) / Winter
  2. Sanguine (Happy) / Spring
  3. Choleric (Angry) / Summer
  4. Phlegmatic (Calm) / Autumn

Nature and the Artist

That the four temperaments would correspond to the four seasons is fitting, for whether people do it consciously or unconsciously we make connections between the face of Nature and our own internal emotional state. Happiness, for example, is typically associated with sunniness and cloudless skies. Thus the seasons are a common object of symbolic use in poetry, and one question hanging over the study of mythology is whether myths such as that of Venus and Adonis are to be taken literally or metaphorically, being potentially a poetic means of expressing spiritual teachings. Shakespeare himself artistically uses the seasons to express how he perceives the presence and absence of his beloved in Sonnet 97, which could just as well have been Venus writing to the deceased Adonis.

How like a winter hath my absence been From thee, the pleasure of the fleeting year! What freezings have I felt, what dark days seen, What old December’s bareness everywhere! And yet this time removed was summer’s time, The teeming autumn, big with rich increase, Bearing the wanton burden of the prime, Like widowed wombs after their lords’ decease. Yet this abundant issue seemed to me But hope of orphans and unfathered fruit; For summer and his pleasures wait on thee, And thou away, the very birds are mute; Or if they sing, ’tis with so dull a cheer That leaves look pale, dreading the winter’s near.

He continues on in this theme in the following sonnet of his sequence, but in this inserts an Alchemical idea similar to what we saw above. Here it is "heavy Saturn" who has been transformed, made light-hearted, laughing and leaping, infused by a youthful spirit. Saturn, the dimmest of the Seven Classical Planets, whose day is last in the days of the week, was linked with the dullest of the Seven Metals in Alchemy, lead. So, Spring has taken a dour and dark metal and made it bright and alive like lightly leaping fire. Here we also see the pairing of red and white in verses that echo what we will see again in the poem 'Venus and Adonis.'

From you have I been absent in the spring, When proud-pied April, dressed in all his trim, Hath put a spirit of youth in everything, That heavy Saturn laughed and leaped with him. Yet nor the lays of birds, nor the sweet smell Of different flowers in odour and in hue, Could make me any summer’s story tell, Or from their proud lap pluck them where they grew: Nor did I wonder at the lily’s white, Nor praise the deep vermilion in the rose ; They were but sweet, but figures of delight Drawn after you, – you pattern of all those. Yet seem’d it winter still, and, you away, As with your shadow I with these did play.

A Brief History

The story of Venus and Adonis was one commemorated in many forms by many peoples of the Near East and Mediterranean tracing back to the cradle of civilization. The earliest known version of the story is the ancient Sumerian myth of Inanna (Lady of Heaven) and Dumuzi (Faithful Son) found inscribed in tablets dating to 2100 - 2000 BCE. Though he was often described as a shepherd, Dumuzi was also known among the Sumerians as "The Bull of Heaven". Inanna and Dumuzi came to be known as Ishtar and Tammuz (Damu) among the Babylonians.

A Lamentation for Tammuz

The god ... great one; lord of the year who sitteth; lord sadly .. Stormy weeping for the mighty one, the god Damu .... Stormy weeping for the youthful one, lord of the name of life. Stormy weeping for the god of the word of judgment, of the eye of precious stones ... Stormy weeping for the god, the artificer, the lord of ..... Stormy weeping for the artificer, the lord of ..... Stormy weeping for him who is the light of my heaven .... The terrific storm has bent him, making him bite the dust. . Source

This couple subsequently came to be known as Astarte and Tammuz among the Phoenicians who exported the pair of deities to Greece along with the Semitic term Adon (Lord). Adon shares the same root as the Hebrew Adonai used for God in the Bible where it also means Lord. This title of course became Adonis among the Greeks. It is unknown where the name Aphrodite came from or even what it means, while Venus, it is known, comes from a Latin word venus which means "love, beauty, charm".

From the Sumerians to the Romans, the pair weren't just the subject of myth but also of annual rites. Among these were the ritual acts of women weeping and beating their breasts in lamentation for the slain deity and the celebration of his resurrection and reunion with his love in a rite known as the Sacred Marriage. In Mesopotamia the Sacred Marriage ritual typically involved a priestess of the Goddess and a king, priest, or priest-king who may have served as actors, or were seen as incarnations of deity, performing the sacred sexual union. In the Adonia festivals of Greece a bridal bed would be prepared with the finest coverings, surrounded with offerings and/or adorned with flowers in a symbolic anticipation of Adonis' return from the dead and temporary reunion with Aphrodite.

Adonis: the Field's Chief Flower

Although the ancient story of Adonis is believed told in the story of a plant or, perhaps more specifically, in a flower which dies as a seed when submerged in soil making way for a new life to spring from the ground, it could be argued that his story is of the annual death and resurrection of the Sun. As Nature's transmutation throughout the year hinges on the relationship between the Sun and the Earth, it is plausible that these are the two original characters of the myth. But we needn't discard one idea for the other.

The Orphic Hymn to Adonis (2nd - 3rd century CE)

Much-named, and best of daimons, hear my prayer, the desert-loving, decked with tender hair; Joy to diffuse, by all desired is yours, much formed, Eubouleos; aliment divine Female and Male, all charming to the sight, Adonis ever flourishing and bright; At stated periods doomed to set and rise, with splendid lamp, the glory of the skies. Two-horned and lovely, reverenced with tears, of beauteous form, adorned with copious hairs. Rejoicing in the chace, all-graceful power, sweet plant of Aphrodite, Love’s delightful flower: Descended from the secret bed divine, of lovely-haired, infernal Persephone. ‘Tis yours to sink in Tartarus profound, and shine again through heaven’s illustrious round, With beauteous temporal orb restored to sight; come, with earth’s fruits, and in these flames delight.

Adonis, Celestial Sun or Terrestrial

Comparing this hymn to the Orphic Hymn to the Sun, similarities are apparent, but regardless, this hymn appears to be referring to the Sun. The reference to Adonis as "Glory of the skies" but also as "Love's delightful flower" is a case where poetic sensibilities may come in handy, for we can simply view the bright bodies in the sky as a field of flowers to reconcile the apparent contradiction, but is that what was meant?

This idea of the Sun as a flower is one mentioned by Zosimos, an Alchemist living in the 3rd to 4th centuries CE, who describes the Sun as a "flower of fire" apparently referring to the primordial Fire which engendered the Universe through copulation with the primordial Waters--which makes a possible link with Eros. Eros (Love) which is the original Greek word used in the above hymn, was in some Greek cosmogonies the Father of all. Hence, "(Eros') delightful flower" in the hymn could likewise be a reference to the Sun. Note that Zosimos in the quote below, doesn't only refer to the Sun as a flower, but also the Alchemist who is the copper that must bloom.

Just as there is a sun, a flower of fire, the celestial sun, right eye of the world; likewise copper, if it blooms (which is to say, if it takes the color of gold) by purification, then it becomes an earthly sun, which is king on earth, as the sun is king in heaven.

--Zosimos of Panopolis

Shakespeare, in his poem, describes Adonis as an earthly sun through the mouth of Venus in a stanza that recalls the sonnet quoted at the beginning and one we will look at later written for his beloved whose bright eye, like the Sun, "gild(s) the object whereupon it gazeth":

The sun that shines from heaven shines but warm, And, lo, I lie between that sun and thee. The heat I have from thence doth little harm; Thine eye darts forth the fire that burneth me, And were I not immortal, life were done Between this heavenly and earthly sun.

Already in the information provided we have reason to believe Adonis is equivalent to the masculine principle of Alchemy which is represented by the Sun, among other things, though rather than being the Father of all things in the mythology which has followed him, there is more reason to believe he is the Son, the Seed of all things.

Venus: Magna Mater

It would sound dubious that the English word matter derives from the Latin word for mother assuming a missing history of how the Universe was created in a Greco-Roman view, or rather, how it was conceived. Indeed, the widespread existence of gendered languages, such as Latin and Greek, stands as evidence to a prehistoric belief that the Universe is the offspring of two primordial parents, and it is their polar qualities that allow for Nature to attain its life-promoting balance. Nature needs both the hot ascending fire and the cold descending rain. And so, various facets of Nature were tied to one of these two archetypes of gender--something we see done explicitly in Alchemy where hot and cold, dry and wet, day and night, summer and winter were seen as but a few of the many faces of the two who conceived the Everything.

A common conception of these primary masculine and feminine elements is 'Father Heaven and Mother Earth'. While these appellations are often interpreted as 'sky and ground' they are more appropriately interpreted as 'spirit and matter' for, in the ancient view, the cosmos had an immaterial and insensible half associated with order, perfection, timelessness, and consistency, and a half perceptible by the senses associated with chaos, imperfection, time, and change which is called 'material' from mater the Latin word for mother. It was these two halves which made up the being we call Universe in a way analogous to a human being, a body possessed of a spirit.

"The spirit of God hovered over the face of the waters" in the Book of Genesis, is a picture of the first two gendered principles in erotic posture just prior to consummating an act of Love. The waters here--and in other religious contexts--represent the soil of creation, a formless substance to be informed by the issued seed, making her the Mother of all things.

The Orphic Hymn to Aphrodite (Venus)

Heavenly, smiling Aphrodite, praised in many hymns, sea-born, revered goddess of generation, you like the nightlong revel and you couple lovers at night, O scheming mother of Necessity. Everything comes from you; you have yoked the world, and you control all three realms. You give birth to all, to everything in heaven, upon the fruitful earth and in the depths of the sea...

At some point in the past, people had gathered that seeds grow by a word or command which is contained within them and they made a connection between this process and the Universe's own germination. So the universal seed was called by Greek philosophers logos spermatikos which means 'seminal word'. This same Word is introduced in the Gospel of John:

In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God in the beginning. Through him all things were made; and nothing that has been made was made but through him. In him (is) life, and that life (is) the light of all mankind. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

What is not present in John is the Word's other half, the feminine princple, the soil of creation from which burgeoned the Tree of Life we call Universe. Krishna says of this tree in the Bhagavad Gita that its roots are above and its branches are below. It is possible that Adonis as "sweet plant of Aphrodite" refers to this same offspring for in some versions of the myth Adonis is not only the lover of the Goddess, but also her son.

The Divine Hermaphrodite

Father Heaven and Mother Earth are common depictions of the primordial parents, but what is found along with this depiction, though less commonly, is the idea that these two are halves of one being. Furthermore there is found the idea that the archetypal or complete human is a hermaphrodite, made in the divine image, both male and female. Perhaps nowhere is this more apparent than in Alchemy where the hermaphrodite is repeatedly used as a symbol for the culmination of the Alchemical process.

Hermaphroditic deities were more common in the ancient world than people might think. An argument that the Book of Genesis describes how God created Adam "male and female" in God's image could be made, but this is for another article. On the same note, we see in Plato's Symposium that the first humans where two-in-one pairs called Androgynes (andro = "man", gyne = "woman") and in Egypt there is a creation myth that describes how the Universe originated from the separation of androgynous deities into male/female couples. The subject is relevant here because the Hermaphrodite is one of the most prominent symbols of wholeness and completeness in Alchemy but too the very term comes from the mythology around Aphrodite and her child.

On left: from 'Quinta Essentia' the Divine Hermaphrodite stands amidst the Wheel of the Zodiac. This symbol of the yearly cycle of the seasons, is instead divided into the four temperaments. The being is flanked on both sides by two viles, one of Sulfur (masculine) and one Mercury (feminine) implying that this wheel of time is itself a hermaphrodite, ascending on one side while it descends on the other.

On right: from 'Theoria Philosophiae Hermeticae' the Hermaphrodite here is named 'Rebis' an abridged form of res binas "combined things". Over the being's head are the symbols of the Seven Classical Planets: Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, and Saturn. Six of the planets are paired by a line with their opposites: Sun/Saturn, Moon/Venus, Mars/Jupiter. Mercury remains alone in the center. Being the messenger of the gods, Mercury was seen as a union of opposites, of Heaven and Earth. While Sun and Moon are typically seen as opposites while Mercury alone possesses both. All of the lunar plaents are on the right side and all of the solar are on the left, and Mercury alone is in the middle just as Mercury's day is in the middle of the week. Not coincidentally, the god Mercury (Hermes) was associated with the "union of opposites".

Not far away was the Greek myth told by Aristophanes in Plato's 'Symposium' of the Androgynes, the first created beings who were two humans fused in one. Though the name Androgyne translates as "man-woman" the Androgynes came in man-man, woman-woman and other combinations according to Aristophanes who uses the myth to explain how sexual relationships are essentially one half of a human looking for its other half. Given the cross-cultural influence of Jews and Greeks, when after describing the creation of mankind the Bible says, "A man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh." the idea between the lines here is that man and woman are partial beings and when they come together they don't just become one, they become whole. The hermaphrodite is the whole being.

Already in the Orphic hymn to Adonis we saw the God referred to as "Female and Male", but so too was Venus/Aphrodite seen as a hermaphrodite in certain contexts--and her child. Cross-gender behavior appears a permanent fixture in Goddess worship from the early days of the Sumerian city-states to the end of the Roman Empire, apparently due to the belief that the deity is both sexes.

There's also a statue of Venus on Cyprus, that's bearded, shaped and dressed like a woman, with scepter and male genitals, and they conceive her as both male and female. Aristophanes calls her Aphroditus, and Laevius says: Worshiping, then, the nurturing god Venus, whether she is male or female, just as the Moon is a nurturing goddess. In his Atthis Philochorus, too, states that she is the Moon and that men sacrifice to her in women's dress, women in men's, because she is held to be both male and female.

--Marcobius, Saturnalia 3.8.2

Salmacis and Hermaphroditus

This story is similar to Ovid's tale of Venus and Adonis but for that the man being chased by an adoring woman, doesn't die, not in the literal sense, nor does a flower grow in the spot where death consumed him. Rather, when Salmacis sees Hermaphroditus bathing in the spring she haunted, being immediately enraptured, she wraps herself around him and prays to the gods that she and the son of Hermes and Aphrodite should never part. The gods grant her request, fusing the two beings into one body, a single being both male and female. One question that appears begging to be asked is: is the merging of male and female into one what Adonis had in store had he chosen to love Venus instead of going hunting? Is that the meaning of the red and white flower that springs from his blood?

A Philosophy of Fire

The myth of Venus and Adonis is seen as a primitive attempt to explain the changing of the seasons, but given what we've seen in the Orphic hymns we could say that the myth is just an observation of the regularly changing relationship between the Sun (Adonis) and the Earth (Venus) turned into a romance, but that leaves the question: why make a myth out of it?

One answer we have seen already: the cycle of the seasons is a natural precedent as parable of spiritual transformation in Alchemy. Evidence that it may have served a similar purpose in the ancient Mediterranean is found in the ancient mystery religions of the area. The mystery religions/cults were initiatory groups which buried spiritual teachings in ceremonies, symbols, and myths, i.e. "mysteries" many of which were shared openly with the public while their true meaning was not. It is from these groups that we get terms such as "mystic/mysticism" and possibly even the word "myth". The only argument to be made for that here is that myths were intimately integrated into mystic rites of initiation in such a way that we could call them fundamental. It was common for mystery religions to be centered on a single myth and these stories typically concerned the subject of death and resurrection or the quest for immortality--the same as Alchemy.

One such myth was that of Demeter and Persephone, the central myth of the Eleusinian Mysteries. Like the myth of Venus and Adonis, this story of a mother and her daughter was linked with the changing of the seasons and was so integral in cult practices that the main rites known as the Lesser and Greater Mysteries were performed on or around the Equinoxes, the times of transition for mother and daughter. The Lesser Mysteries which were open to the public were performed at the Spring Equinox when Persephone emerged from the underworld. The Greater Mysteries, reserved for initiates were performed at the Autumn Equinox when Persephone returned to the realm of the dead. The Eleusinian Mysteries, despite being tauntingly mysterious are among the better known mystery religions. It is known that religious mysteries centering on Venus and Adonis existed, but little is known about them. However, if we take as a given that they existed, we have reason to believe the story of the two lovers served as an initiation myth. In other words, there is a supposed wisdom buried in it as the myth isn't really about Nature's transformation, but about ours.

One thing the myths of Demeter and Persephone and Venus and Adonis have in common besides the impetus for seasonal change, is the fact they center on a love relationship. It's not that Adonis has returned to life that causes plants to grow, but that he has returned to his love.

"What then is Love?" I (Socrates) asked, "Is he mortal?" "No." (Diotima replied) "What then?" "As in the former instance, he is neither mortal nor immortal, but is a mean between the two." Venus is often deemed the Goddess of Love, her only-begotten child in some accounts is Love, and, in some accounts, it was this Love which created the Universe by making love with the primordial Chaos. To love then is an act of worship or devotion to the primal mover of all things, and so it is fitting that in this region and time there appears to be not a straight line between priest(ess) and prostitute. Sacred prostitutes, both men and women, followed the Goddess from her ancient Mesopotamian home as she traveled around the Mediterranean. For many men who served the Goddess, this meant serving in the woman's role.

That of the Immortals did not exist until Eros (Love) had brought together all the ingredients of the world, and from their marriage Heaven, Ocean, Earth and the imperishable race of blessed gods sprang into being. (...) We are the offspring of Eros; there are a thousand proofs to show it. We have wings and we lend assistance to lovers. So, we see the nation of Israel depicted in the Bible as God's wife, in India souls devoted to Krishna are portrayed as women in stories and in paintings, and in Greek myth, Psyche, the personification of Soul, is represented as a woman in Soul's encounter with Love. The myth of Venus and Adonis is but one of many Greco-Roman myths of a certain type: transformation myths which center on a love pursuit. This type of myth is congruent with Alchemy at large, for in general there is no Alchemy without transformation and there is no transformation without Fire.

Venus and Adonis in Ovid's 'Metamorphoses'

'Metamorphoses' is a compendium of Greco-Roman transformation myths in 15 books gathered by the Roman poet Ovid in 8 CE. Written as a poem, Metamorphoses begins with the creation of the Universe as a differentiation of the primordial Chaos into into ordered parts and transitions through loosely connected myths of mortals, immortals, lovers, enemies, and other numerous things culminating in the deification of the Roman Emperor, Julius Caesar. Taken all together a history from the first how a god came to be enthroned upon the Earth could be seen as one big picture of Alchemical transmutation. Not surprisingly, Metamorphoses, was a source of inspiration for Alchemists, as Harvard classicist, Edward Kennard Rand, is quoted in Ovid and the Alchemists

Ovid’s text is made a quarry for the alchemist’s pick and shovel. The fable of Deucalion and Pyrrha, for instance, betokens, like the twin peaks of Parnassus, the masculine and the feminine elements among the metals, that is, gold and silver, from the union of which the philosopher’s stone is produced. In this fashion, the whole poem is subjected to the fatal touch of Midas; Ovid’s gold is converted into the baser metal.

Of course, one of the myths told in Metamorphoses is that of Venus and Adonis, and it is this telling which is believed to be the source of inspiration for Shakespeare fifteen centuries later. There are some key differences. Unlike Shakespeare's version, Ovid's tale of Venus and Adonis has three transformation myths in one. The two tales Shakespeare leaves out are:

  1. Myrrha and Cinyras.

    Story of the mother of Adonis being turned into a Myrtle tree to escape her enraged father who wanted blood after discovering she had deceived him into having sex and impregnating her. Adonis was born from the tree.

  2. Atalanta and Hippomenes

    The story of a race between a woman who refused marriage and a man who was in love with her, racing to win her hand or die trying, then winning by tossing Golden Apples of the Hesperides to lure the woman off track and their subsequent transformation into lions for snubbing the Goddess telling the story to Adonis, Venus.

Though Ovid was not the only ancient author to recount it, the story of Atalanta and Hippomenes was one such taken for Alchemist plunder. The oft-mentioned Alchemical text 'Atalanta Fugiens' by Michael Maier (1617) takes its name (Atalanta Fleeing) from this story and Maier introduces his book with his own distilled rendition of it tinted with Alchemical symbols:

Describing Atalanta's swiftness as "mercurial" reinforces the idea of one fleeing Love, for when extracting mercury by fire's heat from its naturally occurring salt, mercury sulfide, mercury flies up as a vapor while her other half, sulfur, remains. Hence mercury was seen among Alchemists as one who flees the fire, while sulfur (aka brimstone), was seen as like fire. Maier here has mercury eventually yielding to "Sulphur's prudent strength" which all together reinforces the idea of transmutation as a Love pursuit which the Alchemist eventually yields to, being in the woman's role.

Overview of Alchemy

The symbol of chemistry is drawn from the creation by its adepts, who cleanse and save the divine soul bound in the elements, and who free the divine spirit from its mixture with the flesh.

--Zosimos of Panopolis

Alchemy, in brief, is the predecessor of modern science nestled in the belief that the universe is an expression of God's Word, that truths bound in parables abound in Nature from the seldom seen formation of salts to the everyday apparent changing of the seasons. Hence, the tendency in teaching the Art--because it was esoteric--to transmit knowledge in allegory or image using symbolism which had a natural precedent, e.g. fire, salt, gold, seed, etc.. Were it possible to sum the concern of these teachings into a single word, it would have to be transformation or evolution, for as the world turns, it turns out.

So, similar to the way a seed moved by sunlight metamorphoses soil and water into the unearthly loveliness of a rose, so Alchemists sought to transform their own being from something gross (like lead) into some divine thing worthy of show--though there is a key difference. Alchemists saw humans as participants in Creation, and thus made the distinction between Nature and Art as like that between the left hand and the right.

Nature, herself an Alchemist, causes grain to grow from the ground, but for the edible part to be separated from the chaff, ground into flour, and mixed with leaven to make bread requires the intervention of Art. So, while a seed refiguring itself into a plant was used as a religious parable, taking wheat Nature had produced and turning it into bread would be a better illustration of Alchemy's transmutation. So, Alchemy was sometimes called the Royal Art, and the enterprise of bringing the light of a buried deity to life was called the Magnum Opus (Great Work).

In only the last year did I start to study Alchemy and become familiar with its symbols. Though I am no expert, some things have become apparent. One is that there is no canon of Alchemy, but still there are some ideas and corresponding symbols which are consistent, such as the belief in a universal masculine and feminine principle which are often represented by what were conceived of as opposites or gendered pairs: the metals Gold and Silver, the celestial bodies Sun and Moon, the elements Sulfur and Mercury, or a pair of the Four Elements, Fire and Water, sometimes Air (Wind) and Water. They were also represented by the Red King and White Queen, or simply red and white.

The "union of opposites" is a major theme of Alchemy, and for those who practiced it the world was full of opposites. All opposites were initially one within an inchoate and formless mass (the mother) before the germination of the seed began to separate them, and so creation was seen as a descent from unity into division like a tree growing upside down. A tale along these lines comes from Ovid's 'Metamorphoses'--a book about transformation.

Before there was earth or sea or the sky that covers everything, Nature appeared the same throughout the whole world: what we call chaos: a raw confused mass, nothing but inert matter, badly combined discordant atoms of things (...) Nothing retained its shape, one thing obstructed another, because in the one body, cold fought with heat, moist with dry, soft with hard, and weight with weightless things.

The process of transmutation which Alchemists took upon themselves often involved uniting such opposed components of their own being into one, an ascension from division to unity, though what exactly this means is not clear and it should be pointed out that Alchemical texts are often deliberately obscure. The roots of Alchemy are ancient and ideas expressed by Alchemists of Renaissance Europe can be found also expressed by Greek philosophers, Indian gurus, or ancient Chinese sages which is to be expected, for Alchemy as both a spiritual and scientific practice ranged from Asia to Europe and is often found intertwined with religion such as with Taoism in China, and Sufism during the Golden Age of Islam. The origins of Alchemical symbols, however, especially those used in Renaissance Europe onwards, are not so apparent, which could be because, in European history, there is a gap. The Alchemy of the Greco-Roman world seems to have vanished from Europe with the rise of Christianity, though Christianity arguably has a brand of Alchemy all its own.

Almost a thousand years after the birth of Christ, what is commonly called Alchemy was reintroduced to Christian Europe from various influences including the teachings of Muslim philosophers such as Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber) and the discovery of the Corpus Hermeticum in Macedonia. The general philosophical infusion which brought even Greek philosophy back to the region from which it sprang was a ferment in the cultural transformation we call the Renaissance, from the French word for rebirth.

With this rebirth of "classical" ideals and values came a contagious interest in Greco-Roman mythology that lasted for centuries. The Roman myth of Venus and Adonis was not only the subject of Shakespeare's poem, but of many paintings.

A Concoction of Art and Alchemy

Art had a different place in the social landscape of the mid-second millennium than it does now. Whereas art in modern times is mostly seen as an avenue for artists to express their emotions or individual identity, to prove their unique genius, or just to make money, artists in the Renaissance, especially among their patrons, were akin to priests, or, more to the point, instruments of God. In Alchemy, Art is seen as a complement to Nature such that we could say: if Nature is God's left hand, Art is God's right. Though this is Art in the sense of skill or craft, as in the Art of Healing, when it came to the transformation of society, the Arts as we generalize them (painting, music, poetry, etc.) were seen, as Jesus put it, "like leaven which a woman took and measured and kneaded throughout her dough". This perspective is known to have affected works of art (painting) and architecture. Cathedrals, for example, were often built with the belief that the design of the building itself could facilitate what we might call the religious experience.

Many of the paintings done from the 1400s - 1700s at least were commissioned by wealthy patrons to serve as such transformative agents. Hence, many of these paintings have, as their subject, elements of mythology--which arguably serves the same purpose. If one looks, one might find evidence that the same was true for poetry.

One wealthy family in particular, credited with the flourshing of the Italian Renaissance, was the Medici. Cosimo de Medici (1389 to 1464), the founder of the Medici Dynasty was known for his infatuation with exotic philosophical texts coming from outside Christian Europe, notably the Hermetica and NeoPlationist texts. It was he who commissioned Marsilio Ficino to translate the Corpus Hermeticum. Hermeticism and Alchemy are joined at the hip.

Cinnabar is a red salt compound of Sulfur and Mercury mined since prehistoric times. In practical use, cinnabar was valued as the go-to source of the liquid metal, mercury, and ground into a powder as a pigment, commonly known as vermillion, for red paint.

The primary prehistoric use of the mineral was grinding it to create vermillion, and its earliest known use for this purpose is at the Neolithic site of Çatalhöyük in Turkey (7000-8000 BC), where wall paintings included cinnabar's vermillion. Source

Being the product of marriage between an acid and a base, salts in general were seen to depict the union of opposites, but among salts, cinnabar was held up as the prime picture. So, Sulfur and Mercury were also used as symbols for the afore-mentioned king and queen in both Western and Eastern Alchemy, which the Chinese called Yin (Mercury) and Yang (Sulfur). Of course, these two were initially united in one essence called Pure Yang (Cinnabar) before separating into their masculine and feminine counterparts. Whether it was for qualities possessed by its components or by cinnabar itself, that it held high status is clear. In his 'Natural History', the Roman author, Pliny the Elder, states:

(Cinnabar) is of great importance among pigments at the present day, and also in old times it not only had the highest importance but even sacred associations among the Romans. Verrius gives a list of writers of unquestionable authority who say that on holidays it was the custom for the face of the statue of Jupiter himself to be coloured with cinnabar, as well as the bodies of persons going in a triumphal procession, and that Camillus was so coloured in his triumph, and that under the same ritual it was usual even in their day for cinnabar to be added to the unguents used at a banquet in honour of a triumph, and that one of the first duties of the censors was to place a contract for painting Jupiter with cinnabar. For my own part I am quite at a loss to explain the origin of this custom, although at the present day the pigment in question is known to be in demand among the nations of Ethiopia whose chiefs colour themselves all over with it, and with whom the statues of the gods are of that colour.

Ancients knew how to decompose cinnabar for extracting mercury, but also, how to recompose it. Because when cinnabar oxidizes it turns black, it may be the case that splitting blackened cinnabar to its component parts, purifying them, and reuniting them again was seen as revivifying dead cinnabar, and thus a picture of spiritual rebirth and plausible template for the traditional four stages of Alchemy for creating the Philosopher's Stone:

  1. Nigredo (Blackening):

    Cinnabar blackens through exposure to light and humidity

  2. Albedo (Whitening):

    Dead Cinnabar is heated causing Mercury (feminine) to escape as a white vapor which is then purified

  3. Citrinitas (Yellowing):

    Sulfur (masculine), a yellow substance, remains which is then purified

  4. Rubedo (Reddening):

    Cleansed Sulfur and Mercury are merged back together
The significance of Cinnabar's role in Alchemy is more apparent in the East where its name is synonymous with Alchemy. The Chinese term 'Neidan'(內丹) used for esoteric Taoism or Taoist Alchemy is itself composed of two parts: Nei(內 = "inner") and dan(丹) which is often translated as "alchemy" or "elixir" but the online dictionary wordhippo.com gives the definition of 丹 as: red, cinnabar, pellet, powder.

In China, the earliest known use of cinnabar is the Yangshao culture (~4000-3500 BC). At several sites, cinnabar covered the walls and floors in buildings used for ritual ceremonies. Cinnabar was among a range of minerals used to paint Yangshao ceramics, and, at Taosi village, cinnabar was sprinkled into elite burials. Source

It is supposedly for this symbolic significance of cinnabar that the Chinese flag is red. Red in general was seen as the zenith of colors and, like gold, the champion of metals, was linked with the Sun. So, also in Japan red is linked with the Sun, but more specifically with the Shinto Sun-goddess, Amaterasu, who is often depicted in art like the Japanese flag, in red and white.



Cinnabar is is still used for devotional offerings in India where the mineral is known as sindoor. Like the statues of deities painted with cinnabar by the Romans, idols known as linga or lingam which represent the generative principle of the Universe or Shiva as a penis (aka 'shiva lingam') are ritually dosed with sindoor creating an image that emphasizes the association between cinnabar, potency, and marriage.

In addition, the red bindi dot that married women in India wear between their eyes, a symbol of the third eye, and thus of Enlightenment, was for thousands of years painted on with vermilion, known in India as sindoor, itself a symbol of marriage. This tradition began in the Harappan civilisation which existed in India from 3300–1300 BCE and persisted until it was discovered that mercury is toxic.

Bindi is the feminine form of bindu, the white "point" from which the universe germinated in Shaivism described as a white seed (masculine) in red fleshy fruit (feminine), which together are the hermaphrodite deity, Shiva-Shakti.

The description of Shiva-Shakti as a white seed in red fleshy fruit is opposite the typical Alchemical pairing of color and gender, and is also opposite the pomegranate, a fruit with red seeds and white flesh, though the skin is red... Shakespeare's Inspiration and Introduction to the Poem

Shakespeare's poem 'Venus and Adonis' is inaugurated with a Latin quote from the Roman poet Horace:

Vilia miretur vulgus: mihi flavus Apollo Pocula Castalia plena ministret aqua.

Translation:

Let the vulgar crowd admire cheap things; let yellow Apollo Serve me with cups full of Castalian water.

The last part of the quote alludes to the Castalian spring at Delphi where Apollo killed Python and established his oracle. The spring was associated with prophecy and poetic inspiration. Apparently, Shakespare, or the publisher, here is saying he is only concerned with divinely inspired works.

Even if Shakespeare's poem was divinely inspired, it wasn't created ex nihilo. It is believed that Shakespeare based his poem primarily on a tale of the two deities in Ovid's 'Metamorphoses' (8 CE) though he may have been inspired by other stories in the same book for transformation myths centering on a love pursuit is a common motif in the book and in Greco-Roman mythology in general. There are at least 3 transformation myths centering on a nymph or goddess pursuing a man she is in love with in Ovid's Metamorphoses:

  1. Venus and Adonis
  2. Echo and Narcissus
  3. Salmacis and Hermaphroditus
The similarity between the first two gives cause to believe they are variations of the same myth. The third differs in such a way that it can't go without mentioning.

Stanza 2

Stanza 4

‘And yet not cloy thy lips with loath’d satiety But rather famish them amid their plenty Making them red and pale with fresh variety Ten kisses short as one one long as twenty A summer’s day will seem an hour but short Being wasted in such time-beguiling sport’

A peculiar stanza, here Venus is telling Adonis to not make himself sick by overindulging on the sweetness of kissing, but to refrain just enough that his lips become both red and pale, perhaps meant to be a sign of balance in love here. Lips in love poetry are typically red, and so having them "red and pale with fresh variety" seems to have less to do with sexual appeal and more to do with the interaction and intermingling of of the pair, an idea we see again.

Stanza 6

Over one arm the lusty courser’s rein Under her other was the tender boy Who blush’d and pouted in a dull disdain With leaden appetite unapt to toy She red and hot as coals of glowing fire He red for shame but frosty in desire

one point of interest here is the use of "dull" and "leaden" (lead is base largely because it is dull) to describe both Adonis' disdain and his sexual appetite recalling "heavy Saturn" in Sonnet 98. Also, Adonis being "unapt to toy" recalls what Venus said about Mars when she claims credit for teaching the direful god of war "to sport and dance, to toy, to wanton, dally, smile, and jest". Toy in Shakepseare's time meant to be playful, frivolous, or flirtatious. Here the use of red and white is put to new use reinforcing this idea of Adonis was one bashful, unapt to toy, for here red is used to illustrate his shame, white to illustrate his frigidity.

Stanza 13

Still she entreats and prettily entreats For to a pretty ear she tunes her tale Still is he sullen still he lours and frets ‘Twixt crimson shame and anger ashy-pale Being red she loves him best and being white Her best is better’d with a more delight

Once again red is for shame, but now white is tied with anger as Adonis becomes increasingly frustrated. The last two lines are a complete mystery, and the idea expressed at the beginning of the poem of Adonis being white and red seems less to do with his cheeks and lips, and more to do with his nature. Ignoring all other uses, red and white appear in the next to last line to mean shameful and angry as in the line just before. Worth noting that despite being associated with spiritual perfection, red and white as representatives of the masculine and feminine principles have their "negative" qualities, but here, Venus loves them regardless.

Though the tension had been building in the previous stanzas, the conflict has now moved to Venus' face. But no such triumph can take place unless the King imparts great strength and potency to his water and tinges it with his own colour, that thereby he may be consumed and become invisible, and then again recover his visible form, with a diminution of his simple essence, and a development of his perfection.

Stanza 195

By this the boy that by her side lay kill’d Was melted like a vapour from her sight And in his blood that on the ground lay spill’d A purple flower sprung up chequer’d with white Resembling well his pale cheeks and the blood Which in round drops upon their whiteness stood

As pointed out already, this stanza recalls Stanza 2 which describes Adonis as a flower, but furthermore it recalls the very first stanza of the poem which describes the purple-coloured face of the Sun taking its last leave of the weeping morn.

The purple blood on Adonis' face in death is analogous to the purple face of the Sun as it sets, i.e. it is "blood-colored". And, like women of Rome weeping for Adonis, the morn in Shakespeare's poem is "weeping" for the Sun who has "ta'en his last leave". So, not only does the setting sun foreshadow the death of Adonis, but furthermore points to the idea that the myth has its origin in the Sun's cycle, as if Shakespeare is saying: Had Adonis paid attention to the sign in the sky, he wouldn't have gone on the chase.
